The Moda Center Deal.

The Blazers are staying. The arena is getting renovated. Good.

Nobody in this conversation wants the Blazers to leave. The Moda Center should be renovated. That's not the debate.

The debate is about the terms — because right now, Portland is being offered the worst arena deal in the country. It starts with a fact most Portlanders don't know.

Portland owns the Moda Center.

In 2024, the City of Portland bought the arena for $1. The land, the building, everything. Tom Dundon — the Dallas billionaire buying the team for $4.25 billion — will be renting space in a building the city owns.

What landlord renovates a tenant's space for free and charges no rent?

That's not generosity. That's a negotiating failure.

The deal on the table.

  • $871 million in combined public commitments — state $365M, city $405M, county $101M.
  • $0 from Dundon. Not a dollar of private capital for the base renovation.
  • "Less than $600 million" — Wilson's own words for the renovation cost. He called $600M a "placeholder number." The public commits $271M more than the stated cost with no explanation.
  • No rent. Portland owns the building. Nothing charged for the right to operate it.
  • No naming rights sharing. Moda Center naming rights will sell for $10–20M per year. The proposal returns zero to the public.
  • No PILOT payments. Nine of twelve comparable NBA deals include them. This one doesn't.
"Historically, other owners have committed years and put in money… In Portland?"
— Tom Dundon, The Athletic, March 31, 2026. His first day as owner. Asked why he won't contribute money like other owners have.

The relocation threat isn't real.

  • March 25, 2026: NBA Board of Governors voted to begin formal expansion to Las Vegas and Seattle — the only two viable relocation destinations. Both are now off the table.
  • The last attempted NBA relocation was rejected 22–8.
  • Dundon accepted a 20-year lease with relocation penalties in SB 1501 itself.
  • Co-owner Sheel Tyle: "We are committed to Portland, 100 percent. Full stop."
  • Blazers president Hankins, pressed for names of interested cities: "You have cities, not that I can talk to…" He named none. Because both viable cities are in expansion.

Councilor Novick: "I think that's ridiculous. Dundon and his partners just poured $4.2 billion into getting the team. They're not going to leave over a few hundred million dollars. I don't like the idea of being railroaded."

The Glickman Contract.

Named for Dr. Robert Glickman, the Portland physician who negotiated the original $1 purchase of Moda Center. Two components: fair market rent (what Dundon owes for operating a publicly owned building, regardless of who renovates it) and capital recovery (the public's return on $871M). Together: $64M/year — exactly $1M less than Dundon's self-funding cost.

The mayor keeps making our argument.

At his April 8 press conference, Wilson called Portland the Blazers' "landlord" and Moda Center "a pretty good asset to own." He's right on both counts. So why isn't the landlord charging rent?

He also admitted the renovation cost is invented: "It's less than $600 million. We're just using that as a kind of placeholder number." The public commits $871M based on a figure the mayor himself calls a placeholder.

On the city's leverage: "That's just reality." It isn't. Portland owns the building, the team cannot relocate, and Dundon's self-funding cost is $65M/year. The city has every lever. It just has to choose to pull them.

What the Public Records Actually Show

The following connects the documentary record — PRR filings, text messages, internal emails, invoices, and calendar invites — to show how this deal was built, who built it, and what questions remain unanswered.

Finding 1

The PCEF Money Was Already Spent

The Portland Clean Energy Fund was created by voters to fund climate investments in frontline communities — not a general discretionary fund. When Wilson proposed using $75M of it for Moda Center, internal documents from PRR C443668 show the city already knew otherwise.

PRR C443668 — Internal city Q&A for reporters, February 2026
"All current and forecasted PCEF funds are allocated through the Climate Investment Plan through June 30, 2029. Because all PCEF resources are already allocated in the current CIP, commitments in the current CIP would need to be modified in order for PCEF funds to support the Moda renovation effort."
PCEF balance at start of fiscal year: $739,542,176 — all already committed.

Using PCEF for Moda means canceling or deferring existing climate programs — not drawing from surplus. The city knew this while publicly saying PCEF "could potentially" support the renovation. Councilor Clark told constituents the PCEF committee had endorsed Moda; internal records show only an "awareness" site visit occurred. Councilor Novick will not vote for PCEF use — a live obstacle for the city's $405M commitment.

Finding 2

The Mayor Used Different Numbers for Different Audiences — on the Same Day

On February 9, 2026, Wilson went to the Oregon Capitol to lobby for SB 1501. PRR C443668 produced the separate talking points prepared for Democratic and Republican legislators:

For Democrats
City plan of "over $365M" — $125M upfront plus $12M/year.
For Republicans
City plan of "over $260M" — $175M upfront plus $18M/year.

A $105M gap between the two versions — used in the same building on the same day. The public figure ultimately became $405M, a third number different from both. The city was using figures as persuasion tools, not financial facts.

Finding 3

Stafford Sports Had No Authorization for Most of Its Work

The city's financial advisor is Stafford Sports, LLC — a City Attorney Consultant Contract that shields all work product behind attorney-client privilege. The public has never seen any of their financial analysis, deal memos, or negotiating recommendations. The contract was scoped in 2021 for a single task: the bridge lease extension, at a task order value of $15,750.

PRR C454414 — Request for Stafford task orders beyond TO1
Status: Closed — No Applicable Records Available.
Yet Part 6 of the Stafford time sheets (670 pages) shows hundreds of thousands of dollars billed for SB 1501 work, the Charlotte/Raleigh trip, and reviewing the bill's draft during the legislative vote — with no formal task order authorizing any of it.

Unresolved conflict: Stafford's website lists prior work on "Moda Center (Trail Blazers)." The city hired its counterpart's former consultant, made all findings privileged, paid 0.029% of deal value, and no conflict-of-interest disclosure appears anywhere. Carl Hirsh also flew first class on the Charlotte/Raleigh trip, billed to the city as economy.

Finding 4

The Trip Studied Only the Arenas That Proved the Blazers' Point

In December 2025, city staff flew to Charlotte and Raleigh — accompanied by Blazers executives and Stafford's Carl Hirsh, all on the city's dime. They visited only 100% publicly funded arenas. They did not visit Milwaukee, Sacramento, Seattle, or any arena with significant private contribution.

NC_TRIP_ITINERARY_12-2025_FINAL.pdf — stated business purpose
"Strengthening key relationships prior to closing of the Blazers pending sale… learn about experiences of Raleigh's arena owners working with Tom Dundon's ownership group."
Blazers staff were listed as "Confirmed" participants in the original itinerary — not optional add-ons. The city's official PRR response contradicts this directly.
Finding 5

The Blazers Wrote the Pitch Deck the City Used with Legislators

Dan Barrett (Blazers' negotiator) prepared the financial comparable analysis that legislators relied on. He previously served as the lead negotiator for the public in Sacramento and Milwaukee — deals with ~50% private contributions — both of which he excluded from the Portland comparables.

When city staffer Karl Lisle suggested adding Milwaukee and Sacramento, Barrett replied: "As the lead negotiator for the public sector in both Sacramento and Milwaukee, I can say that the market has clearly changed." He used his record of protecting the public in those cities as the reason to exclude those deals from Portland's analysis.

On January 15, 2026, Aisling Coghlan (mayor's chief of staff) distributed the Blazers' own "Rip City Runs Deep" advocacy toolkit to legislators — the city circulated the Blazers' marketing materials as independent analysis.

Finding 6

Beginning November 3, 2025, city officials held weekly Monday meetings under the code name "Project Mt. Hood." PRR C454415 produced the original calendar invite.

Finding 7

The Gift Campaign: Suite Tickets, Tours, and a Letter of Support

Blazers lobbyists Ryann Gleason (CFM Advocates) and Dan Jarman (Crosswater Strategies) ran a coordinated access campaign from before the new council was even sworn in.

November 2024

Jarman obtained OGEC Advice No. 24-537I — pre-authorizing suite tickets before inauguration. OGEC found pre-inauguration electeds aren't "public officials" under ORS Ch. 244. The Blazers timed the event to exploit this window.

January 2025

AAPI Night — Koyama Lane and Kanal invited with complimentary suite tickets.

March 2025 — Women's History Night

Suite invitations to city councilors, Oregon state legislators (including Drazan, Sollman, Pham, others), BOLI Commissioner Christina Stephenson, and Multnomah County Commissioner.

March 2025 — Child ticket dispute

Pirtle-Guiney's office asked to bring her daughter free. City attorney: "While state law allows acceptance of the gift of entertainment for a relative, city administrative rules do not." Pirtle-Guiney's staff asked to "bypass the attorney check-in." She paid $120 for her daughter's ticket.

July 2025

Jarman separately invited Wilson and councilors Pirtle-Guiney, Ryan, and Kanal to a private tour-and-lunch at Moda Center, to brief them on ECOnorthwest's economic impact study — during the months before the council vote.

February 2026

Natalie King (Blazers SVP) thanked Pirtle-Guiney specifically for her letter of support for SB 1501, then requested a follow-up meeting to discuss next steps.

Outstanding question: Were all gifts from the Blazers to Portland officials properly disclosed on required ethics filings? No OGEC disclosure filings for this campaign have appeared in any public records response to date.
Finding 8

The Threats

Between February and March 2026, Blazers lobbyists told at least four Portland city council members their political careers would suffer if the team left Portland — delivered privately, never in public testimony.

PRR C454690 — Text messages, Councilor Koyama Lane, March 2026
"Lobbyists for the Portland Trail Blazers have made it clear that elected officials will be blamed if the team leaves Portland."

These are the same lobbyists simultaneously running the suite ticket access campaign above. Gifts that create access and goodwill, followed by private threats when a vote approaches — a textbook influence operation.

Finding 9

Who Is Tom Dundon?

On March 25, 2026, ProPublica and OPB published internal emails showing Dundon, as CEO of Santander Consumer USA, personally ordered the company to stop requiring proof of income on car loans in 2013. His chief risk officer flagged potential federal law violations in writing. Dundon proceeded.

Oregon's attorney general called the practices "predatory and harmful." Thirty-three states settled for $550M. Dundon left with a $700M separation package — used to buy the NHL's Carolina Hurricanes and now the Trail Blazers.

Oregon AG Dan Rayfield is now separately investigating Exeter Finance — another subprime auto lender where Dundon is chairman, staffed with former Santander executives involved in the original practices.

The Glickman Contract doesn't change whether the deal happens. It changes whether Portland gets paid for it.

  • What is the real renovation cost? The $271M gap between the stated cost and public commitment has never been explained.
  • Were OGEC disclosures filed for the Blazers' gift campaign? No filings have appeared in any PRR response.

  • What are the actual lease terms being proposed? The city has committed $405M but the lease document — which determines what Portland receives — has not been made public.
