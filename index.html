The Blazers are staying. The arena is getting renovated. Good.

Nobody involved in this conversation wants the Blazers to leave. The team is part of Portland's identity. The Moda Center should be renovated into a world-class facility. That's not the debate.

The debate is about the terms—because right now, Portland is getting the worst arena deal in the country.

Here's a fact most Portlanders don't know: in 2024, the City of Portland bought the Moda Center for $1. Portland owns it outright. The Trail Blazers are the tenant. Tom Dundon—the Dallas billionaire buying the team for $4.25 billion—will be renting space in a building the city owns.

The renovation costs $600 million. The combined public commitments total $871 million—state ($365M) + city ($405M) + county ($101M). That's $271 million more than the renovation requires. Dundon's contribution: $0.

The landlord is paying to renovate the tenant's space, charging no rent, and asking for nothing in return. Every other comparable deal in the country includes rent, community benefits, or both.

How this happened.

Public records obtained by KGW and an OPB investigation reveal how the deal was built.

Dan Barrett of CAA Icon—now representing the Blazers—prepared the pitch deck that convinced city and state leaders to fund the renovation publicly. A Portland city staffer, Karl Lisle, pushed back: he suggested adding Milwaukee and Sacramento to the comparisons, because they're similar markets where the team paid roughly half.

Barrett rejected both. His words: "As the lead negotiator for the public sector in both Sacramento and Milwaukee, I can say that the market has clearly changed and these comparables are dated." He then asked city staff to "support the position."

He used his record of protecting the public in those cities to argue against protecting the public in Portland.

The pitch cherry-picked deals where the public paid 90–100%. It left out Golden State ($1.4B private), LA Clippers ($2B private), and Seattle ($1.15B private). It left out Barrett's own Raleigh deal. The "AVERAGE OF 90%" headline was accurate only because the sample was built to produce it.

🛫 City staff took a city-paid 3-day trip to Charlotte and Raleigh with Blazers reps—touring only 100% publicly funded arenas. Not Seattle.

⚠️ Blazers lobbyists told council members that "elected officials will be blamed if the team leaves" and that their "political career would suffer." At least four councilors confirmed this to OPB.

👟 Nike CEO Elliott Hill emailed the mayor: "We all need to do what we can to keep the Trailblazers in Portland. Losing them will be disastrous."

"It's hard for me to believe that they're going to pack up the team and leave tomorrow if we don't commit $75 million from the Clean Energy Fund in the next five minutes. I don't like the idea of being railroaded."
— Councilor Steve Novick, March 2026

The relocation threat is dead.

On March 16, 2026, ESPN reported that the NBA Board of Governors will vote the following week to pursue expansion to Las Vegas and Seattle—at $7–10 billion per franchise. Those are the only two credible relocation destinations. Once they have their own teams, where exactly is Dundon threatening to move? Nashville? It doesn't have an arena.

Meanwhile, NBA Commissioner Silver flew to Portland on March 13 and called the Blazers "part of the fabric of this town," said the league has "50 years of incredible success in this market," and revealed Phil Knight personally called him to say how important it was the team stay. Dundon himself accepted a 20-year lease requirement and relocation penalties in the bill. The last attempted NBA relocation (Sacramento to Seattle, 2013) was rejected 22–8 by the Board of Governors.

The Blazers aren't leaving. The only question is how much Portland pays them to not do something they were never going to do.

The trap nobody's talking about.

Under SB 1501, the lease isn't between Portland and the Blazers. It's between a state-controlled joint authority and the Blazers. The state put in $365 million. The city put in $75–125 million upfront. The state runs the body that negotiates the lease.

The city's only leverage is the moment before it enters. Once the council votes to commit funds, the lease is negotiated by a body the state dominates—and the city is a minority voice at someone else's table.

The council's vote is the negotiation. If they commit funds without attaching binding lease conditions, they will have spent their only card before the game begins.

There's a better way. Seattle already proved it.

In 2017, Seattle issued a competitive bid for its city-owned KeyArena. Two operators competed. OVG won. The result:

🏗️ $1.15 billion private renovation. Zero city money.

🏠 City keeps ownership. 39-year lease. The city collects rent.

💰 All cost overruns absorbed by the operator. Zero taxpayer risk.

Portland can do the same thing. Lease the Moda Center competitively. Let arena operators—OVG, AEG, ASM Global—bid for the right to operate and renovate the building. Dundon can bid too. Multiple bidders competing for a single asset means the city gets the best possible price.

And if the city wants to account for the civic value of keeping the Blazers specifically, there's a standard mechanism for that: a "bidder preference auction." The city names a "Blazers discount"—say, $50 million. The Blazers' bid is treated as that much higher for the purpose of picking the winner. But the actual subsidy is only as large as the gap between the Blazers' bid and the next-best offer. If the gap is $5 million, the city "spends" $5 million—not the full $50 million. The discount is a ceiling, not a floor.

No joint authority. No state intermediary. The city controls 100% of the terms. The Blazers stay. The arena gets renovated. And Portland negotiates as what it is: a landlord with a $600 million building and multiple parties competing to use it.
